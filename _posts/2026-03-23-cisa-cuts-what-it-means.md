There's been a lot of noise about the CISA staffing cuts over the past year, but most of the commentary lands in one of two places: political talking points about the administration's priorities, or vague hand-wringing about "weakened defenses." Neither is particularly useful if you're trying to understand what this actually changes day-to-day for people working in security.

So here's my read on it, from the perspective of someone who spends time thinking about how defenders actually operate rather than how agencies appear in budget documents.

## What Got Cut

The headline numbers are significant enough: the agency dropped from roughly 3,700 employees to around 2,400 over the course of 2025 through a combination of layoffs, buyouts, and departures. The proposed FY2026 budget initially aimed for even deeper reductions, nearly $500 million and over 1,000 positions. Congress softened that to a ~$135 million cut, but the workforce damage was already done before appropriations were settled.

The programs that were eliminated or gutted matter more than the headcount. The Election Security Program is gone entirely, 14 staff, $39.6 million, eliminated. The counter-ransomware initiative was wound down. The Stakeholder Engagement division, which handled outreach to state and local governments, schools, and critical infrastructure operators outside the federal network, was cut to a fraction of its former size. The Joint Collaborative Environment, where CISA synthesized threat data from across the ecosystem, took a $36.5 million hit.

What's notable about that list is what all those programs had in common: they were the parts of CISA that interfaced with people and organizations *outside* the federal government. The cuts didn't primarily touch CISA's work protecting federal civilian networks. They hit the outward-facing mission, the part that actually reached organizations that can't afford their own threat intel teams.

## What This Changes in Practice

For large enterprises with mature security programs, the honest answer is: probably not much, at least directly. Those organizations were never primarily relying on CISA for their day-to-day security posture.

The impact is more pronounced in a few specific areas:

**State and local government.** CISA's regional teams, cut by 27% in funding, hundreds of positions reduced, were often the primary source of cybersecurity support for county governments, school districts, and municipal infrastructure operators. These organizations have small or nonexistent security teams. They relied on CISA assessments, incident response support, and the threat intelligence sharing infrastructure CISA maintained. That safety net is thinner now.

**Threat intelligence sharing.** The Joint Cyber Defense Collaborative and similar programs existed to aggregate threat data that individual organizations couldn't collect themselves. Government entities see attack patterns across federal networks and critical infrastructure that the private sector doesn't have visibility into. Fewer analysts processing that data means slower, less complete intelligence reaching downstream defenders.

**Institutional memory.** This one is harder to quantify but matters enormously. A lot of the expertise that walked out the door in 2025, particularly in ransomware response and critical infrastructure, took years to build. You can rehire headcount, but you can't rehire the institutional knowledge of people who responded to dozens of incidents and built relationships with sector operators over a decade. That's gone.

## The Timing Problem

The cuts happened during a period when the threat environment hasn't gotten quieter. The same week I'm writing this, the FBI and CISA issued a joint advisory about Russian intelligence services running large-scale campaigns against Signal and WhatsApp accounts. March Patch Tuesday addressed 82 CVEs from Microsoft alone. Cisco SD-WAN had an actively exploited authentication bypass with a CVSS of 10.0 sitting in federal networks.

Threat actors don't reduce activity because the defensive agency is understaffed. If anything, signals of institutional weakness invite more aggressive operations.

## The Practical Response

If you're running security for an organization that previously relied on CISA touchpoints, assessments, advisories, incident response support, the responsible move is to start treating that as unavailable and build alternatives:

The CISA KEV catalog and advisories are still publishing, and they're still worth tracking. But the proactive engagement, the assessments, the regional team relationships, those need to be supplemented with other sources now. ISACs for your sector, commercial threat intel feeds, and community relationships with peers in your industry fill some of that gap.

For organizations with any federal nexus, it's worth understanding that the FBI's budget also took a hit, over $560 million and nearly 1,900 staff in the proposed budget. Cyber investigation capacity at the federal level has contracted.

None of this means the situation is unmanageable. Security was never supposed to be dependent on a single agency's health. But the assumptions that underpinned a lot of security programs, that CISA would be there for outreach, assessment, and response support, need to be revisited. The organization that existed two years ago is functionally different from the one that exists today.
